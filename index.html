About this page
What is calibration? Calibration is the alignment between your stated confidence and your actual hit rate. When you say you are 70% confident on a trade, calibration measures how often a 70% prediction actually resolves correctly across many decisions.
Why you need to be calibrated Conviction without calibration is opinion. Position sizing, diversification, and risk all depend on probabilities being honest. A 70% that behaves like 55% is not just wrong — it is expensive. Calibration converts intuition into evidence, and evidence into better-sized bets.
The cost of not tracking Untracked decisions decay into narrative. Memory favors winners; bias rewrites losers. Compounded over many trades, miscalibration is one of the largest sources of underperformance — larger than fees, larger than timing. The error stays invisible until you measure it. Most investors discover their overconfidence only in hindsight, after capital has already been mispriced for years.
How to read this chart Each circle represents a prediction recorded before a trade was placed. Stated confidence is plotted on the X axis. Actual outcome — whether the stock outperformed the market — is on the Y axis.
The dashed diagonal The diagonal represents perfect calibration. When predictions cluster on this line, stated confidence consistently matches real-world accuracy — a 70% prediction resolves correctly 70% of the time.
Overconfident vs underconfident Circles below the line indicate overconfidence — predictions that resolved less accurately than stated. Above the line indicates underconfidence. Most investors trend below.
Position sizing & edge Calibration data enables more precise position sizing — allocating capital based on demonstrated edge rather than perceived conviction. The investor who knows their hit rate is 58% on high-conviction calls sizes differently than one who assumes it is 75%.
