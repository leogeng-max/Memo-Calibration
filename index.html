Suggested prompts
Use these to get moving, not to get to an answer.
The AI accelerates the first hour of research — summarizing filings, surfacing bear cases, mapping competitors. It does not replace reading the 10-K. It will state wrong numbers with full confidence. Treat its output as a junior analyst's first draft: useful, fast, and not to be trusted without verification.
The memo prompt
Paste this to bootstrap a memo. Then paste the data when the LLM asks.

I'm researching AAPL to write a short investment memo. Help me think through it in order. For each question, give me a short, opinionated answer first, then a one-paragraph rationale.

  1. Classification. Is this a Compounder (durable, long runway), an Emerging Compounder (execution risk), a Situational Mispricing (specific outcome bet), or Speculative (no edge)?
  2. Intrinsic value. What's a defensible per-share intrinsic value? Show your assumptions on earnings, multiple, and growth.
  3. Margin of safety. At today's price, is there enough room to be wrong about the details and still be right about the bet?
  4. Thesis. In one sentence, why does this investment work?
  5. Kill line. What specific event, metric, or quarter would prove the thesis broken — not just disappointing?
  6. Probability + horizon. What probability would you assign to the thesis playing out over 3 years, and why that number?
Rule: no financial recall. Numbers — earnings, margins, share count — come from fiscal.ai paste.

About this page
Calibration Calibration measures alignment between stated confidence and actual hit rate. A 70% prediction should resolve correctly 70% of the time across many decisions.
Why calibration matters Conviction without calibration is opinion. Position sizing, diversification, and risk all depend on probabilities being honest. A 70% that behaves like 55% is not just wrong — it is expensive. Calibration converts intuition into evidence, and evidence into better-sized bets.
The cost of not tracking Untracked decisions decay into narrative. Memory favors winners; bias rewrites losers. Compounded over many trades, miscalibration is one of the largest sources of underperformance — larger than fees, larger than timing. The error stays invisible until measured. Most investors discover overconfidence only in hindsight, after capital has already been mispriced for years.
How to read this chart Each circle represents a prediction recorded before a trade was placed. Stated confidence is plotted on the X axis. Actual outcome — whether the stock outperformed the market — is on the Y axis.
The dashed diagonal The diagonal represents perfect calibration. When predictions cluster on this line, stated confidence consistently matches real-world accuracy — a 70% prediction resolves correctly 70% of the time.
Overconfident vs underconfident Circles below the line indicate overconfidence — predictions that resolved less accurately than stated. Above the line indicates underconfidence. Most investors trend below.
Position sizing & edge Calibration data enables more precise position sizing — allocating capital based on demonstrated edge rather than perceived conviction. The investor who knows their hit rate is 58% on high-conviction calls sizes differently than one who assumes it is 75%.
